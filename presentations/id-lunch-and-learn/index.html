Set the analogy down for a second — here's what actually makes the path work

AI is bad at
×
Judgment calls. “Is this good?” gets you whatever sounds plausible.
×
Knowing things. It predicts the next word. Facts are a by-product of training data.
×
Owning outcomes. It can't care whether learners learn.
AI is good at
✓
Executing against specific criteria. Give it a rubric and it'll loop until the lesson meets every row.
✓
Using tools to find information. It's an open-book test. Hand it the book and the prediction gets easy.
✓
Trying things fast. Three prototypes in an hour, not three weeks.
The craft — the judgment, the objectives, the rubric — still has to come from a person. That's why I'm excited to be in a room of IDs: writing specific criteria is already your job.
  • Setting the analogy aside for a second
  • Bad at judgment calls. Ask AI if it's confident, it says yes. Huge bias to being confidently wrong
  • Why: AI predicts the next word you want to hear. It's read thousands of sentences, keeps predicting, checks how right it was. Doesn't actually know or understand — just predicts
  • Bad at knowing things. Because it doesn't know, the information can be very wrong. Often it's right, but who knows really
  • Bad at owning outcomes. Can't care whether learners learn
  • Good at executing against specific criteria. This is the superpower — give it the info and it does things
  • Example: pull up a full paper on bridge, tell it to read, ask a question. The answer is in the article, so prediction is easy and accurate
  • Good at using tools to gather information — Google, docs, files. It can research and synthesize for you
  • Good at doing things fast. Much faster than I can type — I can try, fail, learn, try again
  • Why this is exciting for you: we now have tools for enhanced research — as long as we don't use them to replace research
  • Practical move: have it Google things, but tell it to source its resources and audit everything
  • On criteria: a very clear rubric makes it work well — describe what success looks like

  • Extrapolate this to your own process
  • Frame: can't ask AI if an idea is good or bad. But you can have it summarize user reports — review sites, Reddit — for what people are actually asking about
  • Spec: pretty good at this, but requires multiple iterations. Close collaboration, not outsource
  • Research: great for synthesis and speed. Accuracy isn't great. Always ask “where did you get this from?” — that prompt is very helpful
  • Sequence: avoid judgment calls, but good for bouncing ideas off
  • Build & review: super-fast implementation. Specificity matters — clear criteria for success (in code that's tests; otherwise, a checklist) makes it work
  • Ship & observe: parse logs, cluster reports, summarize metrics — strong. Judgment on “did it succeed?” stays with you

Spaced repetition, retrieval practice, and AI feedback — built from research, powered by objectives

Predict Before You Read
Learners guess what they think is true before seeing the content, activating prior knowledge.
Explain Back
Learners explain the concept in their own words. AI gives Socratic feedback on gaps.
Daily Review
Spaced repetition quiz surfaces past material at optimal intervals for retention.
Practice & Master
Drill sets with mastery gates ensure learners demonstrate understanding before advancing.
  • These are the core concepts I've built into the platform
  • Predict before you read — every lesson asks the learner to engage with the existing information they have
  • Explain back — more engagement. Puts things in their own words
  • Daily review — spaced repetition. Things that are important to remember, not just have exposure to, keep getting exposure. Questions are designed to be fairly easy and encouraging
  • Practice & master (interview) — lets someone really hone their skills and knowledge
